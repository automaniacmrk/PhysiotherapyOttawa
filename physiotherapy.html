Common Physiotherapy Techniques

The majority of techniques used in physiotherapy are hands-on, designed to manipulate, mobilize, and massage the tissues in the body. This can help relieve stiffness and pain, increase blood flow, promote movement, and relax. These techniques include:

  • Massage to stimulate the soft tissues in the body
  • Myofascial and stretching techniques for soft tissue are available.
  • Joint mobilization and manipulation, which use the gliding of the joint to improve motion, function, and control
  • Handcrafted instruments are used for physiotherapy instrument mobilization.
  • Minimal energy techniques (METs) which uses the muscle's own energy to relax the muscles through autogenic or reciprocal inhibition and lengthening the muscle
  • Osteopathy and acupuncture are also available.

Graded Exercise Programmes

You can gradually build your strength, stamina, mobility and activity levels by following a graded exercise programme. Your physiotherapist will show you how to start gently and then gradually increase your activity, without straining yourself or adding to your pain.

Your physiotherapist will probably recommend a combination of:

  • Stretching exercises can help relieve aches and pains, and improve movement in your joints
  • Strengthening exercises for strengthening the muscles supporting your joints
  • General fitness is important for general health.
  • Proprioceptive exercises are a way to improve agility, balance and coordination.

Your physiotherapist will be able to advise on classes which may be available locally such as yoga, t'ai chi or Pilates as well as any walking or sports groups in your area.

Hydrotherapy is a form of aquatic therapy that allows you to do exercises in warm water. Some physiotherapists also have access to this pool. Many people find it easier to move in water - the warmth is soothing and the water supports your weight so that you can move your joints and muscles without straining them.

What is Physical Therapy?

Physical Therapy (PT) is a care that aims to relieve pain and help you function, move and live better. You might need it to:

  • Relieve your pain
  • Improve movement or capability
  • Prevent or recover from a sport injury
  • Preventing Disability or Operation
  • Rehabilitation after stroke, accident, injury, or surgery
  • Balance is key to avoiding falling or slipping.
  • Manage chronic diseases such as diabetes, heart disease, or arthritis
  • Recover after you've given birth
  • Control the bowels or the bladder
  • How to adapt to an artificial limb
  • You will learn how to use an assistive device such as a wheelchair or cane.
  • Grab your brace or splint

Solved: What is the Difference between Physical Therapy and Physiotherapy?

Physical therapy and physiotherapy can be used interchangeably for the majority of people. This means that most people don't know the difference between the two. Many countries use physiotherapy to refer to the practice. However, in the United States, physical therapy is the dominant term and physiotherapy can either describe physical therapy or be used to describe a therapy with little difference to physical therapy. All of this to say, there is little, if any, difference between physical therapy and physiotherapy.

Even so, a small, but notable difference between physical therapy and physiotherapy could be claimed. Some health care workers believe that physiotherapy can be described as a hands-on method of rehabilitation therapy. The physiotherapist employs manual techniques to assist the patient. This could include help with deepening stretches and other approaches. Some would also make the assertion that physical therapy is a largely exercise-based therapy. There are numerous benefits to physical therapy, regardless of any disagreements.

Common Physical Therapy Techniques

Physical therapy is the main method used by physical therapists. They will create exercises that contract, relax and improve mobility for injured or injured body parts, depending on their needs. The goal of these exercises is usually to get back common movements that are painful or impossible to perform. Patients can usually perform the exercises at home once they are comfortable with them.

Sometimes physical therapists may use additional equipment or methods in order to treat patients. Cold and hot therapies such as ice, cold packs, or infrared heating, are available to relieve pain and treat chronic conditions. Because it uses deep heat to decrease inflammation, ultrasound is a common treatment.

Physical therapists who treat acute and chronic pain with TENS units also use them. An electrode is placed near the area of pain with a TENS device. The nerve fibers then send signals to the brain in an attempt to block pain signals.

Pain Relief Treatments

While meds will be helpful, a physiotherapist may also recommend other pain relief methods that can work in conjunction with your medication. You'll be able to continue with some of these treatments yourself between appointments - for example:

  • ice packs to soothe hot, swollen joints
  • heat packs to relax tense, tired muscles
  • splints to support swollen or painful joints
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation), which works by altering pain messages to your brain. A TENS machine is a small electronic device that sends pulses to the nerve endings via pads placed on your skin. Many people find this soothing.

Some physiotherapists may have had additional training in other pain relief techniques, such as:

  • massage or manipulation to reduce stiffness and pain, relax muscles and help to improve the range of movement in a joint
  • Acupuncture is believed to be effective by blocking pain signals from the brain, releasing natural painkillers known as endorphins and thus causing acupuncture to take effect.
  • electrotherapy, where techniques such as ultrasound and low-level laser therapy can help to stimulate the healing process and therefore reduce pain
  • Steroid injections may be an option if your joint is particularly sensitive or making it hard to move.

Role of the physiotherapist

A physiotherapist's role is diverse and not always the same over two days. The physiotherapist might need to evaluate a patient to determine if there are any problems. They may also need to help patients with walking, crutches or other mobility issues.

Education is also an essential part of physiotherapy. Physiotherapists spend a lot of time educating patients, their families and the community to prevent injuries and help people live a healthy lifestyle. A physiotherapist might also be able to plan and execute community fitness programs. If necessary, physiotherapists can also issue sick leave certificates.

During their careers, physiotherapists treat all kinds of people, including children with cerebral palsy, premature babies, pregnant women, rehabilitation workers, athletes, the elderly (to try and get them fitter) and those who need assistance after heart disease, strokes, or major surgery.

What Does A Physical Therapist Do?

A physiotherapist/physical therapist, also known as a PT, focuses on improving your ability to move and function without pain which, in turn, helps boost your quality of life.

The goal of PT is for you to achieve the highest level of movement possible to function in daily life.

Your PT will evaluate your fitness and help you to maintain it.

The following are some examples of physical therapy treatments:

  • An evaluation of pain, flexibility and movement
  • exercises, stretches, or hands-on manipulation to help you get stronger and move better
  • Instruction on posture and how to avoid pain or injury through movement
  • heat or cold therapy and a variety of other modalities to reduce pain
  • A wellness program can improve your overall health.
  • performing wound care
  • treating patients in emergency departments and ICU

You might find it takes only a few sessions to see a PT and you will notice a difference in your health.

You may also need to continue PT for long-term relief. Your PT will evaluate your progress and put together a treatment plan for you.

PTs must earn a doctorate in physical therapy (DPT) before becoming licensed.
